Agent Evaluation / Field Guide

A 90% agent is a 35% product.

Reaching the right answer once is capability: the summit. Doing it every time, across a long climb of plans, tool calls, and steps, is reliability: the descent. Benchmarks celebrate the summit. Production lives or dies on the way down, where small per-step errors compound and most agents quietly fall apart.

~6 min read By Aditya Panchal 4 sources
Base camp

A single success is a capability. A thousand in a row is a product.

Benchmarks measure the summit: whether the system can do the task on a good attempt. That is capability. Production asks a harder question, reliability: if you send it up a thousand times, how many climbers come home? Capability is the model's gift; reliability is mostly the harness's job.

At a single step the two look identical. Over a long climb they diverge brutally. Make an agent 90% reliable per step on a ten-step task, and the odds it finishes are 0.910 ≈ 35%. Nothing got worse; we just asked it not to fall, ten times in a row.

The headline, in one line. A "90% agent" sounds nearly shippable, until you run it across a real, multi-step task. P(finish) = 0.9010 ≈ 0.35 → a 90% step-agent finishes ~35% of ten-step tasks.
An agent that succeeds once has shown you it can. Only one that succeeds again, and again, has shown you it will.

Mountaineers know the asymmetry in their bones: most accidents happen on the descent, not the summit. Agentic systems have the same shape. The demo is the summit; the deployment is the descent. This is a field guide to the climb down, and a companion to the post on biased LLM judges: that one argued your score is biased, this one argues that even an honest score, taken as a single number, lies about production.

The ascent

One number, two completely different stories

Run an agent on a task k times and you can summarize it two ways. pass@k asks whether at least one of the attempts summited. pass^k asks whether all of them did. The same agent looks like a hero by one and a liability by the other, and the gap widens with every extra attempt.

Same agent, two stories. "At least one summit" (pass@k) soars; "summit every single time" (pass^k) sinks. By k=8 they tell opposite tales: ~100% versus ~6%.

Which number is honest depends on what you ship. If you can check the output first (a coding agent whose patch must pass tests), pass@k is fair: you only need one good summit. If the agent acts live with no safety net (support, payments, anything the user sees), pass^k is the truth, because users feel the failures, not the best of three.

On τ-bench, an agent at 61% pass@1 fell to 25% pass^8: across eight runs, the odds of at least one failure neared 75%. Same agent. The leaderboard saw a B-minus; the customer saw a coin flip that loses three times in four.

The death zone

Why the curve dives faster than the math predicts

If every step failed independently, reliability would decay gently and predictably, like a rope losing one strand at a time. Real agents decay faster than that, because their errors are correlated: once a climber takes a wrong turn in the fog, the next steps tend to follow it down.

Within a single climb, each step can fail. The independent-error rope (blue) would hold most of the way; real agents (red) drift, compound, and somewhere past the death-zone line they melt down. The shaded gap is the compounding penalty, and it is invisible on any short task.

Two facts make the death zone real. First, compounding is multiplicative: over an H-step task with per-step error rate ε, success scales as (1 - ε)H, so halving the error rate barely moves a short task but transforms a long one. Second, and worse, errors are positively correlated: a study of long-horizon agents across 20,000+ episodes found decay is super-linear, because once an agent forms a wrong hypothesis it persists instead of recovering. Counterintuitively, the strongest models melt down most, since they attempt the ambitious multi-step strategies that have somewhere to go wrong.

This is why one average misleads: long runs don't just lower the mean, they widen the variance. The same model can be rock-solid on five-minute tasks and erratic on four-hour ones, and only a curve, never a point, shows where that regime begins. METR tracks exactly this with the task-completion time horizon, and tellingly, the length an agent can do reliably (its 80% horizon) runs several times shorter than the length it can do once. That gap is the whole story.

Crevasses

Ten places reliability falls through

The first three are mirages on the way up: measurement choices that flatter the number. The next three are the death-zone mechanisms that actually eat reliability. The last four are why none of it shows up until you are already at altitude.

Group 01

Mirages on the way up

Ways the metric flatters you before the climb even starts.

Best-of-N glory

Reporting pass@k (or quietly keeping the best of several runs) sells one lucky summit as the typical outcome.

"It solved it!" really meant "it solved it once in five tries."

Fix: report pass^k too

Short-hop tasks

Atomic, few-step benchmarks never run long enough for compounding to bite, so they overstate real-world reliability.

Great on 2-step QA, falls apart on a 30-step workflow.

Fix: test long horizons

The flattering average

A single mean hides the spread. 100% on easy plus 0% on hard "averages" to a respectable-looking middle.

"70% success" can mean steady, or perfect-then-useless.

Fix: report variance + worst case
Group 02

The death-zone mechanisms

What actually consumes reliability once the horizon gets long.

Multiplicative compounding

Each step multiplies the survival probability, so success decays as (1−ε)H. Long tasks punish even small per-step errors.

99% per step over 100 steps is only a 37% finish.

Fix: shrink H, decompose

Drift without recovery

Errors are correlated: one wrong turn and the agent keeps walking that way instead of noticing and backtracking.

A bad early assumption quietly shapes every later step.

Fix: re-anchor + verify mid-run

Meltdown / doom loops

Past a point the agent stops making progress and starts flailing: repeating tool calls, re-reading the same files, looping.

Step 22 onward is the same failed action with new phrasing.

Fix: detect early, abort, retry
Group 03

Why you only find out at altitude

Reasons the failure stays invisible — on the benchmark and in the harness — until it's expensive.

Summit fever

The cherry-picked demo run that worked becomes the mental model of the system, and nobody counts the runs that didn't.

The launch video is real; it's also the 1-in-8 take.

Fix: many runs, random seeds

Non-determinism

Temperature, tool-call ordering, and async races mean the same input gives different runs, so the "number" isn't reproducible.

Today's 74% is tomorrow's 68% with no code change.

Fix: pin, seed, repeat

Capability ≠ reliability

A newer model lifts the ceiling (what's possible once) without lifting the floor (what's consistent). Teams ship the ceiling.

"It's smarter now" yet the all-runs success rate barely moved.

Fix: re-measure reliability

Environment confounds

Flaky APIs, rate limits, and non-deterministic tools inject failures the agent didn't cause — yet they're invisible on a clean benchmark and fully visible in production.

The agent was fine; the third-party tool returned a 429 on step 14.

Fix: test with real infra + retries
The descent

Six ways to get the agent home

You don't beat the death zone with a stronger climber. You beat it with discipline: honest measurement, shorter exposed stretches, and a plan for the way down.

Report pass^k next to pass@k

Show consistency beside coverage, at the k that matches your real call volume, and count every run, not the best demo take.

Catches: best-of-N glory, summit fever

Measure long, repeat, and re-measure

Evaluate on tasks the length of the real job (the time-horizon approach), run them many times, and re-run after every model, prompt, or tool change. Compounding only shows up when the climb is long enough to fall on.

Catches: short-hop tasks, non-determinism, capability ≠ reliability

Report the curve, not a number

Show success as a function of task length, plus the spread and the floor (p10, worst-of-N), never a lone mean. An agent you can ship is one whose bad day is acceptable.

Catches: the flattering average

Decompose to shrink the horizon

Break one long task into short, independently verifiable legs with checked handoffs. Cutting H from 30 steps into three verified stages of 10 turns one big multiplicative gamble into three small, contained ones.

Catches: multiplicative compounding

Engineer recovery, not just capability

Reliability is the harness's job: add verification gates between phases, re-anchor to the goal mid-run, and let the agent roll back a bad step. A climber who can self-arrest a slip beats a stronger one who can't.

Catches: drift without recovery

Detect meltdown, set a turnaround rule

Watch for doom-loop signatures (repeated calls, no state change) and, like a climber turning back at a fixed time, give the agent hard budgets and a stop condition so a lost run ends cleanly instead of acting on a corrupted state.

Catches: meltdown / doom loops
