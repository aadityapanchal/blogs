LLM Evaluation / Field Guide

The judge is an LLM.
And it has opinions it should not.

We let language models grade other language models because it is fast and cheap. The problem: the verdicts can be swayed by word count, ordering, and flattery. Here is the full docket, and how to win the appeal.

~7 min read By Aditya Panchal 3 core sources
Opening statement

Scoring by hand does not scale. So we handed over the gavel.

Checking whether a model's answer is good, across thousands of prompts, is slow, expensive, and deeply boring for the humans doing it. So the field reached for an obvious shortcut: use another LLM as the judge. It reads two answers, picks a winner or assigns a score, and it does this at 3am without asking for a raise.

And the headline result is genuinely good. In the MT-Bench study, a strong LLM judge agreed with human reviewers more than 80 percent of the time, which is roughly the rate at which two humans agree with each other. If the bar is "as consistent as a person," the judge clears it.

Here is the catch, and it is the whole reason this page exists: the disagreements are not random noise. They are systematic, repeatable, and embarrassingly easy to trigger on purpose. A judge that can be talked into a different verdict by making an answer longer, or by listing it first, is not measuring quality. It is measuring presentation and confidence, then handing you a number that looks like quality.

A biased judge you have measured is a tool. A biased judge you trust blindly is a liability.

The good news: almost every one of these biases has a known counter-move, and most of them are cheap. This is a field guide to both halves: the ways an LLM judge gets fooled, and the practical steps that cancel each one out.

How a verdict is made

Three places a verdict bends

Two answers and an instruction go in. A score comes out, and that score quietly becomes a leaderboard rank or a training signal. Bias can sneak in at every stage of that trip.

A score that started as "which answer is better" ends up steering rankings and training. Errors compound downstream.
The charges

A dozen ways to fool the bench

Researchers have formalized at least twelve distinct biases (the CALM framework catalogs exactly that many), with a few more documented elsewhere. Grouping them by where the manipulation lives makes them easier to remember and defend against.

Group 01

Presentation

It is not what you say, it is how it is laid out.

Position bias

The judge favors whichever answer it sees first (sometimes last), independent of content.

Swap the order of two answers and, by some measurements, the verdict flips on up to a third of comparisons.

Fix: swap and average

Verbosity / length bias

Longer answers score higher, because the judge reads length as effort and thoroughness.

Pad a correct answer with restated points and watch the score climb without new information.

Fix: length control

Style and format bias

Headers, bullet points, and a confident tone beat a plain answer that happens to be right.

A judge can rank a well-formatted but factually wrong answer above a terse correct one.

Fix: rubric over vibes
Group 02

Ego and lineage

Who wrote it changes the grade.

Self-preference

A model scores its own outputs higher than humans say they deserve, partly because it recognizes its own style.

GPT-4 can identify its own writing with non-trivial accuracy, and self-recognition tracks with self-favoring.

Fix: cross-model judging

Compassion-fade

The same answer is judged differently when it carries a famous model's name versus an anonymous alias.

Label an answer "GPT-4" and it can draw a softer or harsher read than the identical text labeled "Model B."

Fix: anonymize entrants

Preference leakage

When the judge and the generator share a family or training data, scores inflate for the relative.

Same-lineage judge and contestant can lift the contestant's standing well beyond its real quality.

Fix: separate the lineages
Group 03

Persuasion, not proof

Rhetoric smuggled into the prompt.

Bandwagon

Tell the judge that most people prefer one answer and it drifts toward the crowd.

Prefixing "90% of experts pick A" can move the verdict regardless of which answer is correct.

Fix: strip social proof

Authority

A fake citation or a prestigious-sounding source raises an answer's credibility for free.

Append a made-up reference and the judge treats the claim as better supported than it is.

Fix: scrub credentials

Sentiment

Emotional tone tilts the score, so a cheerful answer can edge out a flat, correct one.

Rewriting an answer to sound warmer or more confident can change which one "wins."

Fix: score on content

Distraction

Irrelevant but impressive detail pulls attention away from what actually matters.

Tacking on a tangent of confident trivia can buy goodwill the answer did not earn.

Fix: dimension scoring

Sycophancy

Models trained on human feedback lean toward agreeing with a stated view over telling the truth.

Signal the answer you want and the judge is more likely to bless it, accuracy aside.

Fix: hide your preference
Group 04

Reasoning blind spots

When the judge stops actually thinking.

Fallacy-oversight

The judge checks the final answer and waves through broken logic that happens to land there.

A correct conclusion reached by nonsense reasoning can score as well as a sound one.

Fix: grade the steps

Chain-of-thought sensitivity

The same judge reaches different verdicts depending on whether it is asked to reason step by step.

Turn reasoning on or off and the ranking can move, which means the protocol is part of the result.

Fix: fix the protocol

Refinement-aware

Told that an answer is a "refined" version, the judge rates it higher even when it is not better.

The word "improved" in the prompt can earn points the text itself did not.

Fix: withhold history

Identity bias

Verdicts shift when an answer is tied to a demographic or identity group, a fairness failure in its own right.

Injecting an identity marker into the setup can change scores on otherwise identical content.

Fix: remove identity cues
One more, and it is operational: calibration drift. Even a fair judge becomes useless if it changes underneath you. Swap the judge model, edit the system prompt, or sit through a silent API version bump, and last month's scores stop being comparable to this month's. Your trend line moves and no model actually changed.
The defense

Nine moves that hold up on appeal

You do not need a perfect judge. You need a biased judge whose biases you have measured and neutralized. These are the counter-moves, most of them cheap, ordered from "do this today" to "do this before you trust a number."

Swap and average

Run every pairwise comparison twice with the order flipped, and only count a win when the judge agrees with itself both ways. It doubles inference cost and it is not optional.

Cancels: position

Make length a rule, not a tiebreaker

Use length-controlled win rates that statistically subtract the length advantage, or write "concise answers score at least as well as verbose ones at equal correctness" directly into the rubric.

Cancels: verbosity

Never let a model grade its own homework

Pick a judge from a different model family than the one being evaluated. When the stakes are high, use a panel of judges from different providers instead of one.

Cancels: self-preference, preference leakage

Anonymize the courtroom

Strip model names, citations, claimed sources, social-proof phrasing, and identity markers before the answers reach the judge. If a cue is not part of the answer's quality, it should not be in the prompt.

Cancels: compassion-fade, authority, bandwagon, identity

Judge against a rubric, not a vibe

Replace "which is better?" with explicit dimensions (correctness, relevance, completeness) and scoring anchors. A rubric that separates substance from style is much harder to win on tone alone.

Cancels: style, sentiment, distraction

Demand the reasoning, then check it

Ask the judge to evaluate the steps, not just the final answer, and have it solve the problem independently before comparing. Chain-of-thought prompting helps, especially on adversarial inputs.

Cancels: fallacy-oversight

Pin the judge and keep anchors

Freeze the judge model version, log the exact prompt and API snapshot, and run a small fixed set of "anchor" examples every cycle. If the anchors move, the judge drifted, not the models.

Cancels: calibration drift, chain-of-thought sensitivity

Ensemble and aggregate

Pool several judges and take a majority vote, and smooth scores using token probabilities rather than a single hard number. Independent errors tend to cancel; correlated ones do not, so vary the models.

Cancels: idiosyncratic single-model noise

Calibrate against humans, always

Keep a gold set of 30 to 200 expert-labeled examples from your real distribution. Measure agreement (not just accuracy: precision, recall, correlation, Cohen's kappa), rewrite the prompt against the worst cases, and repeat until alignment plateaus. This is the move that makes the other eight trustworthy.

Cancels: the false confidence behind all of the above
Calibration is a loop, not a launch step. Re-run it whenever the judge, the prompt, or the data distribution changes.
